Maternal health quick reference

Normal pregnancy care
Regular antenatal checkups help detect problems early.
Take prescribed iron, folic acid, and calcium supplements.
Drink enough clean water and eat balanced meals with protein, fruits, and vegetables.
Sleep well and avoid smoking, alcohol, and non-prescribed drugs.

Common mild symptoms
Mild nausea, occasional tiredness, and mild back pain are common in pregnancy.
Small frequent meals can help with nausea.
Rest, hydration, and gentle movement can reduce mild discomfort.

Danger signs that need urgent care
Heavy vaginal bleeding at any time in pregnancy is an emergency.
Severe abdominal pain, severe headache, blurred vision, or swelling of face and hands need urgent medical review.
Fever above 38 C with chills can indicate infection and should be assessed quickly.
Convulsions, fainting, breathing difficulty, chest pain, or persistent vomiting require immediate hospital care.
If fetal movement is reduced or absent after quickening, seek urgent obstetric evaluation.
If water breaks early, especially with fever or bad smell, urgent care is needed.

Blood pressure and preeclampsia awareness
High blood pressure in pregnancy can be dangerous.
Warning symptoms include severe headache, visual changes, upper abdominal pain, and swelling.
Prompt blood pressure check and urine protein evaluation are important.

Gestational diabetes awareness
Increased thirst, frequent urination, and fatigue may need blood sugar screening.
Follow doctor guidance on diet, activity, and glucose monitoring.
Uncontrolled blood sugar increases risk for mother and baby.

Anemia in pregnancy
Anemia can cause fatigue, weakness, and dizziness.
Iron-rich foods include leafy greens, lentils, beans, and fortified foods.
Take iron tablets as prescribed and continue follow-up blood tests.

Medication safety
Do not start or stop medicines without clinician advice.
Avoid self-medication, especially painkillers and herbal mixtures of unknown safety.
For fever or pain, use only pregnancy-safe medicines advised by a doctor.

Hydration and infection prevention
Drink clean water and maintain hand hygiene.
Seek care for burning urination, fever, flank pain, or foul-smelling discharge.
Treat urinary infections early to reduce complications.

Nutrition essentials
Include protein sources like pulses, eggs, dairy, fish, or lean meat where appropriate.
Use iodized salt and include calcium-rich foods.
Take folic acid as advised, especially in early pregnancy.

Rest and activity
Gentle daily activity supports circulation and mood unless doctor advises rest.
Avoid heavy lifting and high-risk physical strain.
Monitor symptoms after activity.

Mental well-being
Anxiety and mood changes can happen in pregnancy.
Seek support from family, community health workers, and clinicians.
If sadness, panic, or hopelessness persists, ask for professional help.

When to call a doctor the same day
Persistent vomiting with poor intake.
Moderate pain not improving with rest.
Less baby movement than usual.
Persistent fever or signs of dehydration.
Any concern that symptoms are worsening.

Post-visit safety reminder
AI guidance is not a substitute for in-person diagnosis.
For severe or rapidly worsening symptoms, go to emergency care immediately.
